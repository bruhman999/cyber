----------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT IS FIBER NETWORKING?
----------------------------------------------------------------
Fiber networking uses light instead of electrical signals
to transmit data through thin strands of glass or plastic.

Why fiber over copper (ethernet)?
  Copper (Cat5e/Cat6):
    Max reliable distance    100 meters
    Max speed                10Gbps (Cat6A)
    Susceptible to           EMI (electromagnetic interference)
    Signal degrades          Over distance

  Fiber:
    Max distance             Hundreds to thousands of meters
    Max speed                100Gbps+ depending on type
    Susceptible to           Physical damage only
    Signal degrades          Much less over distance
    Immune to                EMI completely

Common use cases:
  Data center interconnects
  Server to switch uplinks (10GbE, 25GbE, 100GbE)
  ISP backbone connections
  Building to building connections
  Home lab 10GbE networking on a budget

----------------------------------------------------------------
FIBER CABLE TYPES — MULTIMODE VS SINGLEMODE
----------------------------------------------------------------

MULTIMODE FIBER
  Light travels through a larger core (50 or 62.5 micron)
  Multiple light paths bounce through the core at once
  Cheaper to produce and cheaper transceivers
  Shorter distances — typically up to 550 meters
  Used for: data centers, server rooms, short runs
  Identified by: orange or aqua colored jacket

SINGLEMODE FIBER
  Light travels through a tiny core (9 micron)
  Single direct light path, no bouncing
  More expensive cable and transceivers
  Much longer distances — kilometers to hundreds of km
  Used for: ISP backbones, campus networks, WAN links
  Identified by: yellow colored jacket

QUICK COLOR REFERENCE
  Orange jacket     OM1 or OM2 multimode (older)
  Aqua jacket       OM3 or OM4 multimode (modern)
  Purple jacket     OM4 multimode (some vendors)
  Yellow jacket     OS1 or OS2 singlemode

----------------------------------------------------------------
MULTIMODE FIBER GRADES — OM1 THROUGH OM5
----------------------------------------------------------------

OM stands for Optical Multimode.
Higher number = better performance = higher bandwidth.

  OM1    62.5 micron core    Orange     1Gbps up to 275m
         Oldest standard, rarely used today
         10Gbps limited to 33m only

  OM2    50 micron core      Orange     1Gbps up to 550m
         Improved over OM1
         10Gbps up to 82m
         What you have in your setup (LC to LC OM2)
         Still common in older installations

  OM3    50 micron core      Aqua       10Gbps up to 300m
         Laser optimized fiber
         40Gbps up to 100m
         Most common in modern data centers

  OM4    50 micron core      Aqua       10Gbps up to 550m
         Higher bandwidth than OM3
         100Gbps up to 150m
         Current standard for new installations

  OM5    50 micron core      Lime green 100Gbps+
         Supports SWDM (wideband multimode)
         Future proofed for high density networks
         Most expensive multimode option

YOUR SETUP USES OM2 (ORANGE CABLE)
  OM2 is perfectly fine for 10GbE at short distances
  Most home lab and small office runs are under 82m
  so OM2 handles 10Gbps without any issues
  If you ever need more distance upgrade to OM3/OM4

----------------------------------------------------------------
FIBER CONNECTOR TYPES
----------------------------------------------------------------

LC CONNECTOR (Lucent Connector / Little Connector)
  The most common connector in modern networking
  Small form factor — about half the size of SC
  Uses a push and latch mechanism (clicks in)
  Always comes in pairs for duplex connections
  Used with: SFP, SFP+, SFP28, QSFP transceivers
  What you have: LC to LC patch cable

SC CONNECTOR (Subscriber Connector)
  Older, larger square shaped connector
  Push and pull mechanism
  Less common in new installations
  Still found in older buildings and telecom

ST CONNECTOR (Straight Tip)
  Circular bayonet style connector
  Twist and lock mechanism
  Very old standard, rarely seen today
  Found in legacy campus networks

MPO/MTP CONNECTOR
  Multi-fiber connector — 12 or 24 fibers in one plug
  Used for 40GbE, 100GbE, and high density installs
  Looks like a wide rectangular plug
  Requires special cables and transceivers

----------------------------------------------------------------
LC TO LC EXPLAINED
----------------------------------------------------------------
Your cable is described as:
  OM2 LC to LC Fiber Optic Patch Cable
  Multimode Duplex 50/125 Orange LC LC 1M-15M

Breaking that down:

  OM2          Multimode grade 2, orange jacket
               50 micron core fiber

  LC to LC     Both ends use LC connectors
               One end plugs into your fiber card
               Other end plugs into your switch or
               another device

  Multimode    Uses LED or VCSEL light source
               Cheaper transceivers, shorter distance

  Duplex       Two fibers in one cable jacket
               One fiber transmits (TX)
               One fiber receives (RX)
               Both directions at once (full duplex)
               This is why you see two strands in
               one cable — one for each direction

  50/125       Core diameter / cladding diameter
               50 micron core (the light carrying part)
               125 micron cladding (outer glass layer)
               This is standard for all OM2-OM5 fiber

  Orange       OM1 or OM2 identifier color

  1M to 15M    Available in lengths from 1 to 15 meters

DUPLEX vs SIMPLEX
  Simplex      Single fiber strand, one direction only
               Used for specialized applications
               Rare in standard networking

  Duplex       Two fiber strands, both directions
               Standard for all networking equipment
               TX on one strand, RX on the other
               What you have and what you always want
               for network connections

LC DUPLEX CONNECTOR VISUAL
  An LC duplex connector looks like two LC connectors
  joined side by side in one housing.
  The two fibers are usually color coded:
    One side    Blue or marked A (transmit)
    Other side  White or marked B (receive)
  Polarity matters — if you flip them the link
  will not come up.

----------------------------------------------------------------
TRANSCEIVERS — SFP AND SFP+
----------------------------------------------------------------

WHAT IS A TRANSCEIVER?
  A transceiver is a small pluggable module that converts
  electrical signals from your network card into light
  signals for fiber, or vice versa.
  It plugs into a port called a cage on your NIC or switch.
  Transceiver = transmitter + receiver in one module.

  Without a transceiver your fiber port does nothing.
  The cable plugs into the transceiver,
  the transceiver plugs into the cage on your card.

SFP (Small Form-factor Pluggable)
  Original standard
  Supports up to 1Gbps
  Used for 1GbE fiber connections
  Still common on managed switches for uplinks
  Sometimes called Mini-GBIC

SFP+ (Enhanced Small Form-factor Pluggable)
  The upgrade from SFP
  Supports up to 10Gbps
  Same physical size as SFP
  Most common transceiver in use today
  Used for 10GbE connections
  What you have: Mellanox MFM1T02A-SR SFP+

SFP28
  Supports up to 25Gbps
  Same physical size as SFP and SFP+
  Used in modern high performance servers
  Common in cloud provider infrastructure

QSFP+ (Quad SFP+)
  Four lanes of SFP+ combined
  Supports up to 40Gbps
  Larger module than SFP+
  Used for switch uplinks and spine connections

QSFP28
  Four lanes of 25Gbps
  Supports up to 100Gbps
  Common in modern data center spines

----------------------------------------------------------------
SFP+ TRANSCEIVER TYPES
----------------------------------------------------------------

SR (Short Range)
  Uses 850nm wavelength laser (VCSEL)
  Designed for multimode fiber
  Distance: up to 300m on OM3, 400m on OM4
  Cheapest type of SFP+ transceiver
  What you have: Mellanox MFM1T02A-SR
  The SR at the end means Short Range
  Uses your orange OM2 cable

LR (Long Range)
  Uses 1310nm wavelength laser
  Designed for singlemode fiber (yellow cable)
  Distance: up to 10km
  More expensive than SR
  Used for building to building or campus links

ER (Extended Range)
  Uses 1550nm wavelength laser
  Designed for singlemode fiber
  Distance: up to 40km
  Expensive, used for WAN links

ZR (Long Haul)
  Distance: up to 80km
  Used for metropolitan area networks
  Very expensive

LRM (Long Range Multimode)
  Uses 1310nm wavelength
  Works with older OM1 and OM2 fiber
  Up to 220m on multimode
  Niche use case for older installations

DAC (Direct Attach Copper)
  Not fiber at all but fits SFP+ cages
  Short twinax copper cable with SFP+ on each end
  Distances: 1m to 7m only
  Cheapest way to connect two nearby devices
  at 10Gbps, no transceivers needed separately
  Good for connecting server to top of rack switch

AOC (Active Optical Cable)
  SFP+ connectors with fiber cable attached
  Cable and transceivers are one unit
  Distances: up to 100m
  More flexible than DAC for longer runs
  Cannot replace just the cable or transceiver

----------------------------------------------------------------
COMPATIBILITY RULES
----------------------------------------------------------------

SR transceiver requires multimode fiber (orange/aqua)
LR transceiver requires singlemode fiber (yellow)
Never mix SR with singlemode or LR with multimode

Both ends must match:
  SR to SR      correct
  LR to LR      correct
  SR to LR      will not work

Fiber connectors must match:
  LC to LC      correct (what you have)
  LC to SC      needs an adapter
  SC to SC      correct

Speed must match on both ends:
  SFP+ to SFP+  10Gbps both ends, correct
  SFP to SFP+   1Gbps limited by slower end

Third party transceivers:
  Most switches and cards accept third party modules
  Some Cisco gear requires Cisco branded transceivers
  or a command to override the check:
  service unsupported-transceiver
  Meraki switches may require genuine Cisco SFPs
  for full compatibility and support

----------------------------------------------------------------
FIBER CABLE HANDLING AND CARE
----------------------------------------------------------------

  Always cap fiber ends when not in use
  Dust is the most common cause of fiber link failure
  Never bend fiber sharply — minimum bend radius is
  usually around 30mm or more for patch cables
  Clean fiber ends before connecting with
  fiber cleaning sticks or IPA wipes
  Never look directly into a fiber end when
  a transceiver is active — laser can damage eyes
  even if you cannot see the light (IR wavelength)
  Store extra cables coiled loosely, not kinked
