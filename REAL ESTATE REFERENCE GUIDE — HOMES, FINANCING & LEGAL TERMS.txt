----------------------------------------------------------------
TYPES OF HOMES
----------------------------------------------------------------

STICK BUILT (Site Built)
─────────────────────────
What it is:
  The most traditional and common type of home in the US.
  Built entirely on site from the ground up, piece by piece.
  Called stick built because workers literally stick together
  individual pieces of lumber to frame the house on location.

How it is built:
  Foundation is poured on the property first
  Framing is constructed on site board by board
  Plumbing, electrical, and HVAC installed in place
  Exterior and interior finished on location
  Entire build process happens at the final address

Pros:
  ✅ Highest resale value of all home types
  ✅ Most customizable — can build exactly what you want
  ✅ Easier to get financing (conventional loans)
  ✅ Appreciates in value well over time
  ✅ Perceived as highest quality by buyers and lenders
  ✅ Can be built on any lot or land type

Cons:
  ❌ Most expensive per square foot
  ❌ Longest build time (6 months to 2+ years)
  ❌ Subject to weather delays during construction
  ❌ Labor costs are highest

Cost range:
  $150 to $400+ per square foot depending on location
  and finishes

Best for:
  Buyers who want maximum resale value and customization
  Long term homeowners planning to stay 10+ years

----------------------------------------------------------------

MODULAR HOMES
──────────────
What it is:
  Built in sections (modules) in a factory then transported
  to the property and assembled on a permanent foundation.
  Think of it like building with large Lego blocks — each
  section is built indoors then snapped together on site.
  Once assembled it is essentially identical to a stick
  built home and is treated the same legally.

How it is built:
  Sections built simultaneously in a climate controlled
  factory — walls, floors, roof sections all at once
  Sections transported to the property on flatbed trucks
  Crane lifts sections onto permanent foundation
  Sections joined together and finished on site

Pros:
  ✅ Faster than stick built (weeks not months)
  ✅ Built indoors so no weather delays
  ✅ Often better quality control than site built
  ✅ Treated same as stick built for financing
  ✅ Qualifies for conventional loans and FHA
  ✅ Appreciates like stick built homes
  ✅ Cheaper than stick built in most cases
  ✅ Permanent foundation = real property

Cons:
  ❌ Limited customization compared to stick built
  ❌ Transportation can damage sections
  ❌ Fewer builders to choose from
  ❌ Some stigma from buyers who confuse it with
     manufactured homes (they are not the same)

Cost range:
  $100 to $200 per square foot before land and site work

Best for:
  Buyers wanting a quality home faster and cheaper
  than stick built without sacrificing resale value

Important distinction:
  Modular homes sit on a PERMANENT FOUNDATION and are
  classified as real property just like stick built homes.
  This is different from manufactured homes which can
  be moved and are often on temporary foundations.

----------------------------------------------------------------

MANUFACTURED HOMES (Mobile Homes)
───────────────────────────────────
What it is:
  Built entirely in a factory and transported to a site
  on its own chassis with wheels. Originally called
  mobile homes. Built to HUD federal building code
  instead of local building codes like stick built
  and modular homes.

How it is built:
  Entire home built in factory on a steel chassis
  Transported to site on its own wheels
  Can be placed on a permanent foundation or
  left on temporary piers or blocks
  If on permanent foundation classified as real property
  If on temporary setup classified as personal property

Pros:
  ✅ Cheapest housing option available
  ✅ Can be placed in manufactured home communities
  ✅ Quick to get into housing
  ✅ Some models are surprisingly high quality

Cons:
  ❌ Depreciates like a vehicle unless on permanent
     foundation with land ownership
  ❌ Harder to finance — FHA Title I or chattel loans
     only unless on permanent foundation with land
  ❌ Lower resale value than stick built or modular
  ❌ Some communities do not allow them
  ❌ Often stigmatized by lenders and buyers
  ❌ Insurance can be more expensive
  ❌ If in a mobile home park you rent the land
     which adds monthly cost and instability

Cost range:
  $50,000 to $150,000 for the home itself
  Land separate if you own it

Best for:
  Buyers on very tight budgets needing housing quickly
  Retirement communities and manufactured home parks

----------------------------------------------------------------

CAPE COD
─────────
What it is:
  A style of stick built home originating in New England.
  Compact, one and a half story home with a steep pitched
  roof, central chimney, and symmetrical design.
  Very common in the Northeast United States.

Key features:
  Steep pitched roof designed to shed heavy snow
  Dormers (small windows) cut into the roof line
  Living space often extends into the attic level
  Usually 1.5 stories — full first floor, half second
  Relatively small and efficient footprint

Pros:
  ✅ Very common in New England, easy to resell
  ✅ Efficient use of space
  ✅ Classic look holds value well
  ✅ Sturdy in snow and harsh weather

Cons:
  ❌ Sloped ceilings on upper level limit usable space
  ❌ Upper bedrooms can be hot in summer
  ❌ Dormers expensive to add if not original

----------------------------------------------------------------

COLONIAL
─────────
What it is:
  Two story home with a symmetrical facade, centered
  front door, and evenly spaced windows. The most
  common home style in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic.
  Based on British colonial architecture.

Key features:
  Two full stories with equal square footage per floor
  Symmetrical front exterior
  Formal separate rooms — living room, dining room
  Bedrooms all on second floor typically
  Attached garage common in modern colonials

Pros:
  ✅ Maximum square footage for footprint size
  ✅ Clear separation of living and sleeping areas
  ✅ Very popular and easy to resell
  ✅ Classic timeless look

Cons:
  ❌ Stairs can be difficult for elderly
  ❌ Less open concept than modern ranch styles

----------------------------------------------------------------

RANCH / RAMBLER
────────────────
What it is:
  Single story home with all living space on one level.
  Long horizontal profile, attached garage, open layout.
  Very popular in the 1950s through 1980s and making
  a comeback with aging populations and accessibility needs.

Key features:
  All on one floor — no stairs
  Often wider and longer footprint than two story homes
  Usually has attached garage
  Open floor plans common especially in newer ranches

Pros:
  ✅ Accessible for elderly and mobility impaired
  ✅ Easier to maintain than multi-story
  ✅ Open layout feels spacious
  ✅ Often large yards due to wider lot requirements

Cons:
  ❌ More expensive roof and foundation per sq ft
     since they spread out instead of up
  ❌ Less privacy between bedroom and living areas
  ❌ Larger lot required

----------------------------------------------------------------

SPLIT LEVEL
────────────
What it is:
  Home where floors are staggered at half levels instead
  of full stories. Entry opens to a middle level with
  stairs going both up and down. Very popular in the
  1960s and 1970s in suburban America.

Key features:
  Three levels offset by half a story each
  Entry at middle level
  Living areas on one level, bedrooms on another
  Lower level often partially below grade (underground)

Pros:
  ✅ Good separation between living and sleeping areas
  ✅ Often more affordable than colonials
  ✅ Lower level can be used as family room or office

Cons:
  ❌ Constant stairs between every area
  ❌ Can feel choppy and disconnected
  ❌ Harder to resell than colonial or ranch
  ❌ Not accessible for elderly

----------------------------------------------------------------

TOWNHOUSE / ROWHOUSE
─────────────────────
What it is:
  Multi-story home that shares one or two walls with
  neighboring units. Owner owns the structure and
  sometimes a small yard but shares exterior maintenance
  costs through an HOA. Common in urban and suburban areas.

Key features:
  Two or three stories
  Shared walls with neighbors on sides
  Usually has small front and/or back yard
  HOA covers exterior maintenance in most cases
  Often has attached garage or parking

Pros:
  ✅ More affordable than detached single family
  ✅ Less exterior maintenance responsibility
  ✅ Often in desirable urban locations
  ✅ More space than a condo

Cons:
  ❌ HOA fees add monthly cost
  ❌ Less privacy than detached home
  ❌ HOA rules can be restrictive
  ❌ Shared walls mean noise from neighbors

----------------------------------------------------------------

CONDO (Condominium)
────────────────────
What it is:
  You own the interior unit only. The building, exterior,
  hallways, amenities, and land are owned collectively
  by all unit owners through an HOA. Common in cities
  and resort areas.

Key features:
  Own only the airspace inside your unit
  HOA owns and maintains everything outside your walls
  Monthly HOA fees cover building maintenance, insurance,
  amenities (gym, pool, doorman etc)
  Can be in high rise buildings, mid rise, or garden style

Pros:
  ✅ Low maintenance lifestyle — HOA handles exterior
  ✅ Often includes amenities (gym, pool, concierge)
  ✅ Usually in prime urban locations
  ✅ Lower entry price than single family homes
  ✅ Good lock and leave option for travelers

Cons:
  ❌ HOA fees can be very high ($300-$1000+/month)
  ❌ Special assessments can hit unexpectedly
     (example: HOA votes to replace roof, charges
     each owner $10,000 as a one time assessment)
  ❌ Less control over your property
  ❌ Harder to finance if building has issues
  ❌ Less appreciation than single family in most markets

1031 EXCHANGE
----------------------------------------------------------------
What it is:
  A 1031 exchange (named after Section 1031 of the IRS
  tax code) allows a real estate investor to sell an
  investment property and defer paying capital gains
  taxes by reinvesting the proceeds into another
  like-kind investment property.

  In plain English:
  You sell a rental property that went up in value.
  Normally you would owe capital gains tax on the profit.
  With a 1031 exchange you roll that profit directly into
  buying another investment property and defer the tax
  until you eventually sell without doing another exchange.

Example:
  You bought a rental property for $200,000
  You sell it for $400,000
  Profit = $200,000
  Normal capital gains tax (20%) = $40,000 owed to IRS

  With a 1031 exchange:
  Roll the full $400,000 into a new investment property
  Pay zero capital gains tax now
  Tax is deferred until you sell the new property
  If you do another 1031 at that point defer again
  Some investors defer indefinitely their entire lives
  and heirs receive a stepped up basis at death
  eliminating the deferred tax entirely

Key rules:
  Investment property only — not your primary residence
  Must identify replacement property within 45 days
    of selling the original property
  Must close on replacement property within 180 days
  Must use a qualified intermediary (third party) to
    hold the funds between sale and purchase
  You cannot touch the money yourself during the exchange
  Must be like-kind property (real estate to real estate)
  New property must be equal or greater value to defer
    all taxes — if you buy cheaper you pay tax on
    the difference (called boot)

Common uses:
  Selling a small rental and upgrading to larger one
  Selling multiple small properties to buy one large one
  Moving from residential rentals to commercial property
  Moving from one market to another without tax hit

What it does NOT apply to:
  Your primary residence (use Section 121 exclusion instead)
  Stocks, bonds, or personal property
  Property held for personal use
  Property flipped immediately (must be held for investment)

EASEMENT
----------------------------------------------------------------
What it is:
  An easement is a legal right for someone other than
  the property owner to use a specific portion of that
  property for a specific purpose. The owner still owns
  the land but cannot prevent the easement holder from
  using it for its designated purpose.

  Think of it like this: you own the land but you have
  given (or been required to give) someone else a
  permanent right to use part of it.

Common types of easements:

  Utility Easement
    Most common type. Power companies, water utilities,
    and municipalities have the right to run lines,
    pipes, and equipment across your property.
    You cannot build structures over these areas.
    Example: a strip of your backyard where the electric
    company has the right to access power lines.

  Right of Way Easement
    Gives someone the right to travel across your property.
    Example: a neighbor whose land is landlocked behind
    yours has a right of way easement to drive across
    your driveway to reach the road.

  Ingress/Egress Easement
    Similar to right of way — gives right to enter and
    exit through your property to reach another property.

  Drainage Easement
    Requires you to allow water drainage to flow through
    your property. Cannot block or redirect the drainage.

  Conservation Easement
    Voluntary agreement limiting development on land
    to preserve its natural or agricultural character.
    Owner gives up development rights, often in exchange
    for a tax deduction.

  View Easement
    Prevents you from building structures that would
    block a neighbor's view. Common in scenic areas.

How easements affect you as a buyer:
  They run with the land — when you buy a property
  you inherit all existing easements whether you
  knew about them or not.
  Always review the title report before buying to
  identify all easements on the property.
  Utility easements are extremely common and usually
  not a problem unless you planned to build there.
  Right of way easements can significantly affect
  privacy and property use.

Where to find them:
  Title search / title insurance report
  Property deed
  County recorder's office
  Survey of the property

EQUITY AS DOWN PAYMENT
----------------------------------------------------------------
What it is:
  Using the existing equity in a property you already own
  to fund the down payment on a new property purchase
  instead of using cash savings.

  Equity = Current market value of your home
           minus what you still owe on the mortgage

  Example:
  Your home is worth $400,000
  You owe $250,000 on the mortgage
  Your equity = $150,000

  You can tap that $150,000 to use as a down payment
  on a new property without selling your home.

Methods to access equity for a down payment:

  HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit)
    A revolving line of credit secured by your home equity
    Works like a credit card against your home value
    Draw what you need when you need it
    Variable interest rate typically
    You only pay interest on what you draw
    Best for: buying an investment property while keeping
    your current home as a rental

  Home Equity Loan
    A lump sum loan against your equity
    Fixed interest rate and fixed payment
    Receive all money at once
    Best for: when you know exactly how much you need
    for the down payment upfront

  Cash Out Refinance
    Refinance your existing mortgage for more than
    you currently owe and take the difference as cash
    Example: owe $250,000, refinance for $320,000,
    receive $70,000 cash to use as down payment
    Replaces your current mortgage with a new one
    Best for: when you want one loan at potentially
    better rate instead of two separate loans

  Sale of Current Home
    Sell your current home first, use the equity proceeds
    as down payment on new home
    Simplest approach but requires temporary housing
    between selling and buying
    Best for: buyers moving to a new primary residence

Important considerations:
  Using equity adds debt — you are essentially borrowing
  against your home to fund another purchase
  If property values drop you could be underwater
  on both properties simultaneously
  Lenders will count the new HELOC or equity loan
  payment in your debt to income ratio which affects
  how much you qualify for on the new mortgage
  Interest on HELOCs and home equity loans used for
  investment property may be tax deductible
  Consult a tax advisor before using equity this way

Example of equity as down payment in action:
  Own a home worth $500,000, owe $200,000
  Equity = $300,000
  Take out HELOC for $80,000 (20% of $400,000 purchase)
  Use $80,000 as down payment on $400,000 rental property
  Now own two properties
  Pay HELOC monthly on top of both mortgages
  Rental income ideally covers investment property mortgage
  and contributes toward HELOC payment
